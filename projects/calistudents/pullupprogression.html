Step by step guide to achieving your first pull-up

The pull-up is one of the most important exercises for your upper body - not many exercise come close to the benefits of the pull-up.

Whether your main form of exercise is weight training or calisthenics - or perhaps you just work out at home when you have the time - the pull-up should be included in your workout - it's essential for all levels of fitness.

With that being said - it is a more "advanced" exercise simply because of the strength needed to perform them with good form.

Luckily for everyone there is an abundance of progression exercises you can do to replace - and eventually build your way up to - the standard pull-up.

Let's begin with the first progression:

The Australian pull-up

No - despite its name you won't be hanging upside down - so don't get too excited.

The Australian pull-up is the perfect progression exercise because you can increase or decrease the difficulty level simply by changing your foot placement.
Simply put - your grandma can follow along.

Keep at these until you can do 10 repetitions of the smallest incline - And don't worry if you think you're ready and it ends up being too hard - just go back to the previous progression. No shame in that.

Dead Hangs

Dead Hangs are a great way to get you used to hanging on the bar - and they train your forearms and grip a lot.
They also lead directly into the next progression:

Scapula Shrugs

For the scapula shrugs you want to hang in a straight line with a tight grip and your knuckles facing up.
You're basically going from a dead hang to an active hang - and then back again.

→

Retract your scapulas - and then relax them. Do this until you can perform 10 repetitions.
Then go on to the next progression:

Active Hang

Just like you did in the previous progression - retract your scapulas - but instead of relaxing them afterwards you keep them retracted.

Keep working at this until you can hold an active hang for a 45 full seconds.

The jump negative pull-up

This is going to be the last progression before the standard pull-up.

The jump negative pull-up sounds a little confusing - but don't worry - it's pretty simple.

→

→

The idea is you use the momentum from your jump to get to the top position of a pull-up - and come back down as slow as you can.

And then you jump again.

Keep doing jumping negative pull-ups - as you get better and stronger you'll be able to use less and less momentum.

This will eventually turn into your first pull-up.
